                1. Explain the process of photosynthesis. 
		Answer: Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy, usually from the sun, into chemical energy stored in glucose. This process occurs in the chloroplasts of plant cells, where chlorophyll captures light energy. The light energy is used to convert carbon dioxide from the air and water from the soil into glucose and oxygen. The overall chemical equation for photosynthesis is: [ 6CO_2 + 6H_2O + light \ energy \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 ] Photosynthesis is crucial for life on Earth as it provides the primary source of energy for nearly all organisms and releases oxygen into the atmosphere.

		2. Discuss the causes and effects of global warming. 
		Answer: Global warming refers to the long-term increase in Earth’s average surface temperature due to human activities, primarily the emission of greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and nitrous oxide (N₂O). These gases trap heat in the atmosphere, leading to the greenhouse effect. Major causes include the burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, and natural gas), deforestation, industrial processes, and agricultural practices. The effects of global warming are widespread and significant. They include rising sea levels due to melting polar ice caps and glaciers, more frequent and severe weather events such as hurricanes and droughts, changes in precipitation patterns, and disruptions to ecosystems and biodiversity. Additionally, global warming can have serious impacts on human health, agriculture, water resources, and economies.

		3. Describe the structure and function of the human heart. 
		Answer: The human heart is a muscular organ located in the chest cavity, slightly to the left of the midline. It is roughly the size of a fist and consists of four chambers: two atria (upper chambers) and two ventricles (lower chambers). The right atrium receives deoxygenated blood from the body through the superior and inferior vena cavae and pumps it into the right ventricle. The right ventricle then pumps the blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery for oxygenation. Oxygenated blood returns to the left atrium through the pulmonary veins and is then pumped into the left ventricle. The left ventricle, which has the thickest walls due to its role in pumping blood throughout the body, sends oxygen-rich blood into the aorta and subsequently to the rest of the body. The heart’s function is to maintain a continuous flow of blood, delivering oxygen and nutrients to tissues and removing waste products. This process is regulated by electrical impulses that coordinate the heart’s contractions.

		4. Analyze the impact of the Industrial Revolution on society. 
		Answer: The Industrial Revolution, which began in the late 18th century, had profound impacts on society. It marked a shift from agrarian economies to industrialized and urbanized ones. Key developments included the invention of machinery, the rise of factories, and advancements in transportation and communication. The positive impacts of the Industrial Revolution included increased production and efficiency, economic growth, and the creation of new jobs. It also led to technological innovations and improvements in living standards for many people. However, the Industrial Revolution also had negative consequences, such as poor working conditions, child labor, and environmental pollution. The rapid urbanization led to overcrowded cities with inadequate infrastructure, contributing to health problems and social inequalities. Overall, the Industrial Revolution significantly transformed societies, economies, and the environment.
		
		5. Explain the theory of evolution by natural selection. 
		Answer: The theory of evolution by natural selection, proposed by Charles Darwin, explains how species evolve over time through the differential survival and reproduction of individuals with advantageous traits. According to this theory, individuals within a species exhibit variation in their traits, some of which are heritable. In a given environment, certain traits may confer a survival or reproductive advantage, making individuals with those traits more likely to survive and reproduce. Over generations, these advantageous traits become more common in the population, leading to evolutionary change. Natural selection acts on the variation within a population, favoring traits that enhance fitness. This process can lead to the adaptation of species to their environments and, over long periods, the emergence of new species. The theory of evolution by natural selection is supported by extensive evidence from various scientific disciplines, including genetics, paleontology, and comparative anatomy.
